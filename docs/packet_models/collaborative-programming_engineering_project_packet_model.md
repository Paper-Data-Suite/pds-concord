# Collaborative Programming or Engineering Project Packet Model

**Status:** Draft for contract discovery
**Project:** Paper Data Suite
**Module:** `pds-concord`
**Location:** `docs/packet_models/`
**Date:** July 13, 2026

## 1. Purpose

This document models one representative `pds-concord` packet for a collaborative programming or engineering project.

Its purpose is not to plan the project, teach a development methodology, manage source code, or produce final printable forms. It tests the Concord conceptual design against a longer-duration activity involving:

* several work sessions;
* changing and overlapping responsibilities;
* dependencies among students and subteams;
* iterative design;
* external digital or physical products;
* milestone reviews;
* testing and troubleshooting;
* contribution evidence accumulated over time;
* shared and individual outcomes; and
* teacher scoring based on several sources.

The model assumes that:

* the teacher has already planned the project;
* project requirements and subject-specific directions exist before Concord is used;
* class and roster data exist through `pds-core`;
* Concord generates the paper artifacts used to coordinate and document collaborative work;
* students and teachers complete those artifacts by hand;
* completed pages are scanned and filed;
* Concord does not inspect source-code repositories, engineering files, or digital edit histories automatically;
* Concord does not perform handwriting recognition, OMR, transcription, or AI analysis;
* the teacher reviews evidence and records scores manually;
* extended written explanations belong to `pds-quillan`; and
* machine-readable checks belong to `pds-scoreform`.

## 2. Baseline Project Scenario

The representative case assumes a group of three to five students completes a programming or engineering project over several class sessions.

Possible examples include:

* designing and programming a small game;
* building a physical computing prototype;
* developing a simple application;
* constructing and testing a mechanical device;
* designing a bridge or structure;
* creating a robotics solution;
* producing a data-analysis tool;
* or completing an interdisciplinary design challenge.

The project includes:

* interpretation of requirements;
* selection of an initial approach;
* division of work;
* coordination among dependent tasks;
* iterative construction;
* testing;
* troubleshooting;
* revision;
* milestone review;
* presentation or demonstration;
* and final evaluation.

The packet should also be adaptable to shorter design challenges and longer project-based learning units.

## 3. Activity Structure

### 3.1 Activity

A project activity is associated with:

* one class;
* one project brief or challenge;
* one or more project groups;
* several work sessions;
* one or more milestones;
* optional subteams;
* teacher-selected collaborative criteria;
* optional standards references from `pds-core`;
* optional ScoreForm assignments;
* optional Quillan assignments; and
* references to external project artifacts.

The project plan, technical requirements, content instruction, and schedule remain teacher- or curriculum-owned.

### 3.2 Session

A session represents one class period or project work period.

A session may focus on:

* requirements analysis;
* research;
* design;
* prototyping;
* implementation;
* testing;
* debugging;
* revision;
* documentation;
* preparation for presentation;
* or final demonstration.

Group membership, responsibilities, and subteam arrangements may change between sessions.

### 3.3 Milestone

A milestone is a teacher-defined checkpoint within the project.

Examples include:

* proposal approved;
* design selected;
* prototype completed;
* minimum viable product functioning;
* test plan completed;
* revision completed;
* final product submitted;
* and presentation delivered.

A milestone is not necessarily a separate session. One session may contain several milestones, or one milestone may span several sessions.

### 3.4 Group and subteam

A project group may divide temporarily into subteams.

Examples include:

* interface and logic;
* hardware and software;
* research and construction;
* design and testing;
* documentation and presentation;
* or two teams building separate components.

Subteams must remain linked to the parent project group and should be time-bounded.

### 3.5 Roles and responsibilities

Possible roles include:

* project coordinator;
* lead programmer;
* interface designer;
* hardware builder;
* researcher;
* tester;
* debugger;
* documentation lead;
* quality reviewer;
* presentation lead;
* materials manager; and
* integration coordinator.

A role is a named project function. A responsibility is a specific task, deliverable, decision, or obligation.

Students may:

* hold more than one role;
* share one role;
* change roles;
* contribute outside their assigned role; or
* temporarily assist another subteam.

## 6. Artifact A: Project Responsibility and Dependency Map

### 6.1 Purpose

The Project Responsibility and Dependency Map documents:

* project tasks;
* assigned responsibilities;
* relationships among tasks;
* students or subteams responsible;
* expected outputs;
* required handoffs;
* and changes made as the project develops.

It is not the project plan itself. It records how the planned project work is distributed and coordinated.

### 6.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** group recorder, several group members, or teacher;
* **Subject:** project group, members, tasks, and dependencies;
* **Scope:** group and multi-subject.

### 6.3 Suggested paper structure

Possible sections include:

| Task or component | Responsible student or subteam | Depends on | Output or handoff | Status | Reassigned to |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------ | ---------- | ----------------- | ------ | ------------- |

The form may also include:

* milestone;
* start session;
* target completion session;
* supporting contributors;
* blocking issue;
* integration point;
* and teacher approval when required.

A visual dependency map may use:

* boxes for tasks;
* arrows for dependencies;
* student initials;
* milestone markers;
* and handoff symbols.

### 6.4 Scoring use

The artifact may provide evidence of:

* coordination;
* task distribution;
* recognition of dependencies;
* communication;
* responsibility;
* and adaptation.

Assignment alone is not evidence of completion.

A student should not receive credit merely because a task was assigned to them, nor lose credit for a task reassigned for legitimate reasons.

### 6.5 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* project tasks as referenceable entities;
* responsibilities linked to students or subteams;
* dependencies among tasks;
* expected outputs;
* handoff relationships;
* reassignment;
* status over time;
* and a distinction between responsibility assignment and contribution evidence.

## 7. Artifact B: Design Decision Record

### 7.1 Purpose

The Design Decision Record documents consequential choices made by the group.

Examples include:

* selecting one algorithm;
* choosing a structural design;
* deciding among materials;
* changing a user interface;
* selecting a data structure;
* abandoning an approach;
* reducing project scope;
* or resolving a conflict between requirements.

The record should capture important decisions, not every minor preference.

### 7.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** group recorder, several contributors, or decision owner;
* **Subject:** group decision, component, or milestone;
* **Scope:** group/event.

### 7.3 Suggested paper structure

| Decision | Alternatives considered | Evidence or criteria | Contributors | Final choice | Consequence |
| -------- | ----------------------- | -------------------- | ------------ | ------------ | ----------- |

Additional fields may include:

* date or session;
* component;
* unresolved concern;
* dissenting position;
* teacher approval;
* and later revision reference.

### 7.4 Decision status

A decision may be:

* proposed;
* accepted;
* provisional;
* rejected;
* superseded;
* deferred;
* or reversed.

A later decision should not silently replace the original record.

### 7.5 Scoring use

The artifact may provide evidence of:

* reasoning;
* use of criteria;
* comparison of alternatives;
* responsiveness to evidence;
* collaborative decision-making;
* and revision of thinking.

A technically unsuccessful decision may still demonstrate strong collaborative reasoning if the choice was reasonable based on available evidence.

### 7.6 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* event-level decision records;
* alternatives;
* contributors;
* decision status;
* superseding relationships;
* component or milestone context;
* and possible disagreement without forcing artificial consensus.

## 8. Artifact C: Work-Session and Milestone Log

### 8.1 Purpose

The Work-Session and Milestone Log provides a concise chronological record of project progress.

It should document meaningful work without requiring students to record every action.

### 8.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** rotating recorder, project coordinator, several members, or teacher;
* **Subject:** group, session, and milestone;
* **Scope:** group/session.

### 8.3 Suggested paper structure

Each session entry may include:

* session date or number;
* current milestone;
* work completed;
* work still in progress;
* blocked work;
* students or subteams involved;
* decisions made;
* help requested;
* next actions;
* and recorder.

A compact format might be:

| Completed | In progress | Blocked | Next actions | Responsible students |
| --------- | ----------- | ------- | ------------ | -------------------- |

### 8.4 Milestone review

At a milestone, the group or teacher may record:

* met;
* partially met;
* not met;
* revised;
* deferred;
* or no longer applicable.

The reason should remain distinguishable from the status.

### 8.5 Scoring use

The log may provide evidence of:

* sustained progress;
* coordination;
* reliability;
* communication;
* adaptation;
* and follow-through.

It should not reward groups merely for producing more paperwork.

### 8.6 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* chronological entries;
* session references;
* milestone references;
* status changes;
* blocked work;
* next actions;
* changing contributors;
* and continuation across several pages or sessions.

## 9. Artifact D: Testing, Debugging, and Revision Log

### 9.1 Purpose

The Testing, Debugging, and Revision Log documents how the group evaluates and improves its product.

Depending on subject, it may record:

* failed test cases;
* bugs;
* structural weaknesses;
* prototype failures;
* unexpected behavior;
* measurement problems;
* usability issues;
* material limitations;
* revision attempts;
* and final outcomes.

### 9.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** tester, debugger, builder, programmer, group recorder, or several contributors;
* **Subject:** project component, product, test, or revision event;
* **Scope:** group/event/component.

### 9.3 Suggested paper structure

| Test or problem | Expected result | Actual result | Evidence | Proposed change | Responsible student | Outcome |
| --------------- | --------------- | ------------- | -------- | --------------- | ------------------- | ------- |

Optional fields include:

* component;
* version or iteration;
* severity;
* reproduced by;
* teacher consulted;
* regression introduced;
* and linked decision record.

### 9.4 Version context

Concord should not attempt to manage software versions or engineering files.

It may store human-entered references such as:

* iteration 2;
* prototype B;
* build 4;
* code version tested;
* or file label.

These are contextual references, not source-control records.

### 9.5 Scoring use

The artifact may provide evidence of:

* systematic testing;
* persistence;
* evidence-based revision;
* communication;
* technical contribution;
* quality control;
* and response to failure.

The existence of bugs or failed prototypes is not itself negative evidence.

### 9.6 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* component references;
* test events;
* expected and actual outcomes;
* version or iteration labels;
* responsible contributors;
* revision relationships;
* and circumstances outside student control.

## 10. Artifact E: Contribution and Handoff Record

### 10.1 Purpose

The Contribution and Handoff Record documents work that passes between students or subteams.

It is especially useful when:

* one component depends on another;
* one student begins work another completes;
* a subteam produces an input for another subteam;
* code, materials, research, or designs are transferred;
* or integration work is required.

### 10.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** contributing student, receiving student, recorder, or several participants;
* **Subject:** contributor, recipient, task, component, and handoff;
* **Scope:** multi-subject/event.

### 10.3 Suggested paper structure

| Contributor | Work or component | Recipient | What was handed off | Completion state | Follow-up needed |
| ----------- | ----------------- | --------- | ------------------- | ---------------- | ---------------- |

Additional fields may include:

* session;
* dependency;
* acceptance or verification;
* problem discovered after handoff;
* supporting contributors;
* and teacher note.

### 10.4 Contribution categories

Contributions may include:

* implementation;
* construction;
* design;
* research;
* testing;
* debugging;
* documentation;
* explanation;
* organization;
* integration;
* peer support;
* resource preparation;
* and quality review.

The system should not privilege visible production over less visible coordination or testing.

### 10.5 Scoring use

The record may provide evidence of:

* contribution;
* reliability;
* communication;
* integration;
* dependency management;
* and support for others.

The recipient's acceptance of a handoff does not automatically prove quality, and the contributor should not be solely blamed for a later integration failure without context.

### 10.6 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* several student subjects;
* contributor and recipient roles;
* task or component references;
* handoff events;
* verification status;
* dependencies;
* and contribution categories.

## 11. Artifact F: Teacher Project Observation Tracker

### 11.1 Purpose

The Teacher Project Observation Tracker allows the teacher to record targeted evidence across several groups, subteams, milestones, and sessions.

It must remain usable while the teacher:

* answers technical questions;
* monitors materials;
* approves milestones;
* checks progress;
* conferences with groups;
* and resolves project obstacles.

### 11.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** teacher;
* **Subjects:** several students, groups, subteams, components, or milestones;
* **Scope:** multi-subject and multi-group.

### 11.3 Suggested paper structure

Possible sections include:

* group and subteam roster;
* session;
* milestone;
* selected criteria;
* brief individual notes;
* group-process notes;
* observed contribution type;
* obstacle or dependency;
* support provided;
* follow-up required;
* not observed;
* insufficient evidence;
* absent;
* and excused.

### 11.4 Candidate criteria

Possible criteria include:

* completes useful work;
* communicates progress;
* manages dependencies;
* contributes to decisions;
* tests or verifies work;
* responds to feedback;
* supports integration;
* adapts when plans change;
* assists another member;
* and fulfills responsibilities.

### 11.5 Contract implications

This artifact reinforces the need for:

* one page with several students and groups as subjects;
* session and milestone context;
* subteam references;
* contribution categories;
* teacher-support records;
* and links from one source artifact to several later scores.

## 12. Artifact G: Group Retrospective

### 12.1 Purpose

The Group Retrospective records a concise evaluation of the team's collaborative process at a milestone or at the end of the project.

It may focus on:

* what helped the group progress;
* what caused delays;
* how responsibilities changed;
* which handoffs succeeded or failed;
* how decisions were made;
* what contribution was least visible;
* what should change during the next phase;
* and what remains unresolved.

It is not an extended project report.

### 12.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** group recorder, several members, or group consensus;
* **Subject:** group and project phase;
* **Scope:** group.

### 12.3 Suggested paper structure

Possible formats include:

* plus/delta;
* start/stop/continue;
* milestone retrospective;
* dependency review;
* contribution-balance check;
* successful handoff and failed handoff;
* hidden contribution;
* next-phase adjustment;
* and consensus status.

### 12.4 Scoring use

The retrospective may support:

* formative group feedback;
* group-process scoring;
* responsibility adjustments;
* teacher conferencing;
* and planning of later project phases.

It should not automatically assign the same score to every member.

### 12.5 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* milestone or phase context;
* group authorship mode;
* consensus status;
* references to tasks, handoffs, or decisions;
* and optional group-score linkage.

## 13. Artifact H: Teacher Scoring Rubric

### 13.1 Purpose

The Teacher Scoring Rubric records criterion-level judgments after the teacher reviews project evidence.

It separates scoring from project coordination and evidence collection.

### 13.2 Author and subject

* **Author/scorer:** teacher;
* **Subject:** one student, one subteam, or one group;
* **Scope:** individual, subteam, or group.

### 13.3 Possible individual criteria

Individual criteria might include:

* completes useful work;
* fulfills responsibilities;
* communicates progress and obstacles;
* manages handoffs;
* contributes to design decisions;
* tests or verifies work;
* responds to feedback;
* supports integration;
* assists peers appropriately;
* and adapts when responsibilities change.

### 13.4 Possible group criteria

Group criteria might include:

* coordinates dependent tasks;
* makes reasoned design decisions;
* maintains progress across milestones;
* tests and revises systematically;
* integrates components;
* distributes work responsibly;
* responds to obstacles;
* and completes the shared product.

### 13.5 Evidence references

A score may reference:

* Project Responsibility and Dependency Map;
* Design Decision Record;
* Work-Session and Milestone Log;
* Testing, Debugging, and Revision Log;
* Contribution and Handoff Record;
* Teacher Project Observation Tracker;
* Group Retrospective;
* external project artifacts;
* ScoreForm results;
* Quillan responses;
* or teacher professional judgment.

### 13.6 Score is not grade

Concord may record:

```text
Responsibility fulfillment: 3 of 4
Dependency communication: 4 of 4
Testing and revision: 3 of 4
Group integration: 17 of 20
```

These are scores.

Concord does not determine project weighting or course grades.

## 14. Artifact I: Artifact Cover Sheet

### 14.1 Purpose

The Artifact Cover Sheet associates external project work with Concord.

Possible attached artifacts include:

* printed source code;
* screenshots;
* diagrams;
* schematics;
* CAD printouts;
* circuit diagrams;
* test results;
* photographs of prototypes;
* presentation slides;
* planning boards;
* physical-model photographs;
* and teacher-provided project forms.

### 14.2 Author and subject

* **Author:** group recorder, contributing student, or teacher;
* **Subject:** external artifact, component, group, or milestone;
* **Scope:** artifact/group/component.

### 14.3 Suggested paper structure

The cover sheet may include:

* artifact title;
* artifact type;
* project component;
* group;
* contributors;
* version or iteration;
* milestone;
* related Concord artifacts;
* number of attached files or pages;
* location of digital original;
* and teacher filing confirmation.

### 14.4 External digital products

Concord should store a reference or teacher-entered location when practical.

It should not:

* clone repositories;
* inspect commit histories;
* parse project files;
* infer authorship from digital metadata;
* or replace source-control and project-management systems.

### 14.5 Contract implications

This artifact requires:

* external artifact references;
* file or location references;
* contributor lists;
* version labels;
* component and milestone links;
* attachment relationships;
* and privacy classifications.

## 15. External Reference J: Quillan Project Explanation

Extended project writing belongs to Quillan.

Examples include:

* design rationale;
* technical explanation;
* individual reflection;
* project postmortem;
* explanation of a failed approach;
* defense of a design choice;
* user documentation;
* and written evaluation of collaboration.

Concord may store:

* Quillan assignment ID;
* whether the response is individual or group-authored;
* relationship to the project or milestone;
* whether the response may inform Concord scoring;
* and which Concord criteria it complements.

Concord should not reproduce Quillan's writing workflow.

## 16. External Reference K: ScoreForm Individual Check

ScoreForm may handle structured individual accountability.

Examples include:

* concept checks;
* terminology checks;
* procedure checks;
* interpretation of test results;
* understanding of project requirements;
* or structured self- and peer-ratings using OMR.

Concord may store:

* ScoreForm assignment ID;
* relationship to the project;
* milestone or session;
* whether the result informs an individual score;
* and which Concord component references it.

Concord should not perform OMR.

## 17. Packet Generation Model

### 17.1 Teacher configuration

The teacher supplies:

* class;
* project activity ID and title;
* project group membership;
* sessions;
* milestones;
* optional subteams;
* roles or responsibilities;
* selected Concord templates;
* selected collaborative criteria;
* scoring scale;
* privacy settings;
* optional standards profile and standard IDs;
* optional ScoreForm references;
* optional Quillan references;
* and expected external artifacts.

This is project instrumentation, not project planning.

### 17.2 Generated artifact instances

For a group of four students completing a multi-session project, Concord might generate:

* one Project Responsibility and Dependency Map;
* one Design Decision Record with continuation pages;
* one Work-Session and Milestone Log;
* one Testing, Debugging, and Revision Log;
* one Contribution and Handoff Record;
* one Group Retrospective per milestone;
* four individual Teacher Scoring Rubrics;
* one group Teacher Scoring Rubric;
* and Artifact Cover Sheets as needed.

Each scannable page receives:

* artifact instance ID;
* page-level QR code;
* human-readable fallback code;
* template ID and version;
* activity and session or milestone context;
* group, subteam, or student labels as appropriate;
* and page number.

## 18. QR and Routing Requirements

### 18.1 Shared Core contract

Concord must use the shared `pds-core` QR and routing contract.

This project case requires support for:

* group-level artifacts;
* subteam-level artifacts;
* individual scoring pages;
* multi-subject contribution records;
* event-level decision and handoff records;
* session and milestone context;
* external artifact cover sheets;
* continuation pages;
* and teacher-authored multi-group trackers.

### 18.2 Required routing concepts

The shared payload or linked local record must resolve:

* module;
* class;
* activity or assignment-routing ID;
* session;
* milestone;
* group;
* subteam when applicable;
* artifact instance;
* packet instance;
* template and version;
* document type;
* page number and total pages;
* author or authorship mode;
* subject scope;
* student ID when applicable;
* component or task when useful;
* and privacy classification.

### 18.3 Avoided workarounds

Concord must not:

* represent groups or subteams as synthetic students;
* place a group ID into `student_id`;
* treat the recorder as the sole contributor;
* infer digital authorship automatically;
* encode full project details in QR payloads;
* or create a Concord-only QR grammar.

## 19. Scan and Filing Workflow

### 19.1 Intake

The teacher may scan:

* one group's packet;
* several milestone packets;
* teacher observation pages;
* attached printed artifacts;
* screenshots;
* diagrams;
* or mixed class batches.

PDS Core retains the readable source scan before Concord-specific processing.

### 19.2 Concord processing

Concord:

* splits source files when necessary;
* reads page QR codes;
* identifies artifact instances;
* routes pages to the project, session, milestone, and group;
* associates cover sheets with external artifacts;
* preserves source provenance;
* flags duplicate, missing, or conflicting pages;
* and presents evidence for teacher review.

Concord does not interpret handwriting or digital project contents.

### 19.3 Longitudinal organization

The filing model must preserve:

* session order;
* milestone order;
* decision revisions;
* task reassignment;
* handoffs;
* testing iterations;
* and superseded records.

A later artifact may supersede an earlier decision or plan without erasing the historical record.

## 20. Review Workflow

The teacher reviews artifacts for:

* correct class;
* correct project;
* correct session or milestone;
* correct group or subteam;
* correct authors and subjects;
* scan readability;
* page completeness;
* attachment completeness;
* contribution attribution;
* responsibility changes;
* privacy;
* relevance;
* and readiness for scoring.

### 20.1 Contribution review

The teacher may distinguish:

* assigned responsibility;
* claimed contribution;
* corroborated contribution;
* observed contribution;
* disputed contribution;
* incomplete contribution;
* reassigned work;
* and contribution prevented by dependency failure.

### 20.2 Handoff review

The teacher may review:

* what was expected;
* what was delivered;
* whether the receiving student could use it;
* whether clarification was needed;
* whether later problems were caused by the handoff;
* and whether the issue was reasonably foreseeable.

### 20.3 External artifact review

Concord may record that an external artifact was:

* present;
* missing;
* incomplete;
* inaccessible;
* replaced;
* or not applicable.

It does not evaluate the artifact automatically.

## 21. Scoring Model

### 21.1 Individual score targets

Possible individual targets include:

* useful contribution;
* responsibility fulfillment;
* communication;
* dependency management;
* design reasoning;
* testing and revision;
* technical or material work;
* documentation;
* integration;
* peer support;
* and response to feedback.

### 21.2 Subteam score targets

Possible subteam targets include:

* component completion;
* internal coordination;
* communication with the parent group;
* successful handoff;
* testing;
* and integration readiness.

### 21.3 Group score targets

Possible group targets include:

* coordination across dependencies;
* quality of design decisions;
* sustained progress;
* integration of components;
* testing and revision;
* adaptation;
* and completion of the shared product.

### 21.4 Evidence-to-score relationship

A score may reference:

* several Concord artifacts;
* one or more external project artifacts;
* a teacher observation tracker;
* a ScoreForm result;
* a Quillan response;
* or teacher professional judgment.

Individual, subteam, and group scores may rely on overlapping evidence while remaining separate judgments.

### 21.5 Missing and exceptional states

The scoring workflow should support:

* absent;
* excused;
* not observed;
* insufficient evidence;
* not applicable;
* blocked by dependency;
* responsibility reassigned;
* project scope changed;
* milestone deferred;
* external tool failure;
* and score deferred.

These states should not automatically become low scores.

## 23. Exception Cases

### 23.1 Student absence

An absent student may have:

* no responsibility for that session;
* a responsibility reassigned;
* an alternate contribution;
* an excused status;
* no score;
* or a later individual task.

### 23.2 Group membership change

A student may join or leave a project group.

Earlier records must retain the original membership.

### 23.3 Subteam creation or dissolution

Subteams may exist only during certain sessions or milestones.

They should not become permanent groups in the class roster.

### 23.4 Responsibility reassignment

A task may be reassigned because of:

* absence;
* time constraints;
* difficulty;
* teacher intervention;
* dependency failure;
* or project redesign.

The original assignment and reason for reassignment should remain visible.

### 23.5 Dependency failure

One student's work may be delayed because another component is unavailable.

The system must separate blocked work from failure to contribute.

### 23.6 Shared digital account

Several students may work through one device or account.

Digital account ownership must not be treated as definitive authorship.

### 23.7 Unequal visible production

One student may write code or construct the visible product while others perform testing, research, design, coordination, or debugging.

Concord must support these less visible contribution types.

### 23.8 Failed prototype or software defect

Failure of the product does not automatically indicate poor collaboration.

The response to failure may provide stronger evidence than the final outcome.

### 23.9 Project scope change

The teacher or group may reduce or redirect the project.

Earlier responsibilities and milestones may become superseded or not applicable.

### 23.10 External artifact inaccessible

A linked file, repository, or project may be unavailable during review.

Concord should record the access problem without fabricating evidence.

### 23.11 Conflicting contribution claims

Students may disagree about who completed or supported a task.

Concord preserves the claims and the teacher's moderation decision.

### 23.12 One artifact, several phases

A log may continue across several sessions or milestones.

The page sequence and chronology must remain explicit.

## 26. Decisions Supported by This Model

The programming and engineering case supports these provisional decisions:

1. Concord activities must support several sessions and milestones.
2. Groups may contain temporary subteams.
3. Tasks and dependencies are distinct from roles and responsibilities.
4. Responsibilities and contributions must remain separate concepts.
5. Concord must represent handoffs between students or subteams.
6. Blocked work must remain distinct from incomplete or neglected work.
7. Design decisions may be revised or superseded without erasing history.
8. Testing, failure, debugging, and revision are valid collaborative evidence.
9. External digital products should be referenced, not automatically inspected.
10. Digital account or repository ownership is not reliable evidence of sole authorship.
11. Individual, subteam, and group scores may draw from overlapping evidence.
12. Contribution categories must include technical, organizational, testing, integration, and support work.
13. Extended project writing belongs to Quillan.
14. OMR-based accountability checks belong to ScoreForm.
15. PDS Core's QR contract must support group, subteam, milestone, task, event, and multi-subject Concord artifacts.

## 27. Open Questions

1. Should tasks, milestones, and components be first-class Concord records or configurable labels inside project artifacts?
2. Is a subteam a temporary group record or a distinct project-specific structure?
3. How much dependency modeling is appropriate before Concord begins to resemble project-management software?
4. Should a handoff be its own record or an entry within a contribution log?
5. How should the system represent shared responsibility without diluting individual accountability?
6. Should students be allowed to submit individual contribution claims that are not confirmed by the group?
7. What moderation workflow is appropriate for conflicting contribution claims?
8. How should external project locations be stored without assuming Git, Google Drive, or another platform?
9. Which version or iteration fields are useful without creating source-control functionality?
10. Should one teacher tracker produce derived evidence records for several students and milestones?
11. How should scores distinguish contribution quality from product quality?
12. Should a blocked student receive a status, a score, or both?
13. Which collaboration criteria should ship as starter templates for programming and engineering projects?
14. Should milestone packets be separate packet instances or parts of one long-lived project packet?
15. How should completed projects be handed to future reporting or archival modules?
