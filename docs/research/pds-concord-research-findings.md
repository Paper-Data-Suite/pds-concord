# pds-concord Research Findings: Classroom Discussion and Collaborative Work

**Status:** Research synthesis for pre-contract design  
**Date:** July 11, 2026  
**Project:** Paper Data Suite — `pds-concord`

## Purpose

This document summarizes research and practical guidance relevant to a Paper Data Suite module for documenting classroom discussions, group work, and collaborative learning. Its purpose is to inform later decisions about data contracts, schemas, workflows, and software architecture.

It is not yet a specification. The recommendations intentionally leave room for different subjects, grade levels, teaching philosophies, activity types, accessibility needs, and grading policies.

## Executive Summary

The research supports the following conclusions:

1. **Putting students in groups is not sufficient.** Productive collaboration depends on task structure, shared goals, individual accountability, explicit expectations, and opportunities for groups to reflect on how they worked.
2. **Group products, collaborative processes, and individual learning are different constructs.** Concord should be able to document each without collapsing them into one score.
3. **Discussion quality matters more than speaking frequency.** Useful evidence includes reasoning, questioning, connecting ideas, responding to others, clarifying, synthesizing, facilitating participation, and advancing the group's work.
4. **Participation is multimodal.** Speaking is one form of participation, but students may also contribute through listening, writing, organizing, researching, drawing, manipulating materials, documenting, questioning, or synthesizing.
5. **Students can collect valuable evidence.** Self-assessment, peer feedback, group reflection, contribution logs, and artifact references can reveal work a teacher did not directly observe.
6. **Student-generated evidence requires structure and moderation.** Peer and self-assessment work best when criteria are explicit, students are trained, evidence is descriptive and specific, and the teacher retains authority over consequential decisions.
7. **No single evidence source is sufficiently reliable for a high-stakes judgment.** Important decisions should be based on multiple observations, sources, or artifacts whenever practical.
8. **Not observed is not the same as not demonstrated.** The system must preserve uncertainty rather than converting missing evidence into a negative rating.
9. **Assessment should be activity-specific.** A Socratic seminar, science lab, programming team, debate, and short partner task should not be forced into one universal rubric.
10. **Concord should be an evidence and reflection system, not a surveillance engine.** Audio recording, transcription, automated talk analysis, and algorithmic scoring should not be required or treated as authoritative.
11. **The final evaluative decision belongs to the teacher.** Concord may organize evidence, calculations, and summaries, but it should preserve provenance and distinguish evidence from interpretation and grading.
12. **Privacy should be built in.** Student-to-student judgments are sensitive. Collection should be minimal, confidential by default, locally controlled, and visible only to appropriate users.

## 1. Scope and Terminology

The literature uses *collaborative learning*, *cooperative learning*, *group work*, *discussion*, and *dialogic teaching* in overlapping but not identical ways.

For Concord, a practical umbrella definition is:

> **Collaborative activity:** an activity in which two or more students coordinate ideas, actions, or resources toward learning, problem solving, discussion, or a shared product.

This includes:

- partner and small-group work;
- seminars, debates, and whole-class discussion;
- laboratories and design challenges;
- collaborative writing and research;
- project teams;
- peer review;
- presentations;
- role-based simulations;
- shared problem solving; and
- informal, short-duration collaboration.

Three levels should remain conceptually separate:

1. **Group outcome:** What the group produced, decided, solved, explained, or accomplished.
2. **Collaborative process:** How members interacted, coordinated, reasoned, and supported the work.
3. **Individual evidence:** What a particular student contributed, understood, practiced, or learned.

A polished group product does not prove equitable participation or individual understanding. Conversely, a group may collaborate productively while producing an imperfect outcome because the task was difficult. Concord should preserve these distinctions.

## 2. What Effective Collaborative Learning Requires

Research reviews generally find positive academic and attitudinal effects from well-designed cooperative or collaborative learning, but outcomes vary by task, context, age, subject, and implementation. The Education Endowment Foundation accordingly distinguishes structured collaboration from ordinary, unstructured group work and emphasizes that students need support and practice.

A useful organizing framework from social interdependence theory identifies five recurring conditions:

- **Positive interdependence:** members have a meaningful reason to work together;
- **Individual and group accountability:** both the group and its members remain responsible for learning and work;
- **Promotive interaction:** members explain, question, assist, and advance one another's efforts;
- **Interpersonal and small-group skills:** collaboration skills are taught rather than assumed; and
- **Group processing:** members reflect on what helped, what hindered, and what should change.

These principles should guide activity design and evidence collection, but they should not become mandatory fields or a universal scoring rubric. For example, assigned roles can create interdependence in one activity but be unnecessary or counterproductive in another.

### Design implications

Concord should support teachers in defining:

- the activity's academic and collaborative purposes;
- the expected group outcome;
- what requires interdependence rather than simple task division;
- what each student must individually demonstrate;
- which collaborative behaviors matter for this activity;
- which evidence sources will be used;
- whether evidence is formative, summative, or informational; and
- when the group will reflect and revise its process.

## 3. What Should Be Documented or Assessed

Concord should not assume that every construct below is assessed in every activity. Teachers should select a small, relevant subset.

### 3.1 Discussion and collective meaning-making

Potential dimensions include:

| Dimension | Observable evidence |
|---|---|
| **Substantive contribution** | Offers an idea, interpretation, solution, example, or relevant information that advances the work |
| **Reasoning and evidence** | Explains thinking, supports a claim, tests an inference, or identifies limitations |
| **Inquiry** | Asks authentic questions, requests evidence, probes assumptions, or opens a productive line of thought |
| **Connection and responsiveness** | Refers to, extends, challenges, or combines another person's contribution |
| **Listening and interpretation** | Accurately represents another idea, responds to its substance, or checks understanding |
| **Clarification** | Defines terms, resolves ambiguity, restates a problem, or makes an idea more understandable |
| **Synthesis** | Identifies patterns, reconciles positions, summarizes progress, or formulates a group conclusion |
| **Facilitation and inclusion** | Invites contributions, creates space, keeps discussion productive, or helps the group hear overlooked perspectives |
| **Revision of thinking** | Changes, qualifies, or strengthens a position in response to evidence or dialogue |

These dimensions are preferable to raw talk counts because they describe how a contribution functions. Frequency may still be recorded as descriptive context, but it should not automatically imply quality, engagement, or learning.

### 3.2 Collaborative work processes

Potential dimensions include:

| Dimension | Observable evidence |
|---|---|
| **Task contribution** | Completes useful work, supplies needed information, or creates part of the shared product |
| **Coordination and reliability** | Communicates status, meets commitments, manages dependencies, and helps sequence the work |
| **Mutual support** | Explains, checks understanding, shares resources, or helps another member succeed without taking over |
| **Decision-making** | Helps compare alternatives, establish criteria, reach decisions, or document rationale |
| **Conflict and disagreement** | Addresses ideas rather than persons, surfaces disagreement constructively, and helps resolve impasses |
| **Role fulfillment** | Performs an assigned function when roles are part of the activity |
| **Role flexibility** | Adapts when the group's needs change rather than treating a role as a fixed identity |
| **Monitoring and adaptation** | Notices problems, evaluates progress, seeks feedback, and adjusts the plan |
| **Ethical contribution** | Attributes sources and contributions accurately and does not misrepresent others' work |

### 3.3 Outcomes and individual accountability

Concord should also support evidence concerning:

- quality of the shared product or performance;
- completion of the shared goal;
- individual explanation or demonstration of learning;
- individual reflection on contribution and learning;
- transfer to a new problem or context;
- fulfillment of a specifically assigned responsibility; and
- growth across repeated collaborative experiences.

### 3.4 Contextual indicators

Some commonly recorded variables are useful context but should not be treated as universal measures of collaboration:

- number of spoken contributions;
- time spent speaking;
- attendance or presence;
- preparation;
- time on task;
- completion of a named role;
- number of edits, notes, or artifacts;
- seat location or group proximity; and
- teacher visibility of the student.

These can help interpret evidence, but each can be misleading when used alone.

## 4. Evidence Sources

No source is perfect. Concord should retain the source, author, subject, time, criterion, and review status of each record.

| Evidence source | Strengths | Risks and limitations | Recommended use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Teacher observation** | Professional judgment; can interpret context and instructional goals | Teacher cannot observe all groups continuously; brief observations may miss important work | Targeted observations across multiple moments; criterion-specific notes |
| **Student self-assessment** | Promotes reflection and reveals invisible planning, listening, or learning | May be inflated, overly harsh, vague, or shaped by students' understanding of criteria | Formative reflection, evidence selection, goal setting, and explanation |
| **Peer observation or assessment** | Peers see work the teacher misses; assessing others can deepen understanding of criteria | Friendship, conflict, popularity, retaliation, collusion, bias, and inconsistent standards | Structured, private, criterion-based feedback; moderated before consequential use |
| **Group reflection** | Captures shared account of process, decisions, and needed changes | Can conceal disagreement or produce a socially acceptable consensus | Group processing and planning; preserve whether response was consensus or individual |
| **Contribution log** | Provides a chronological record of tasks, decisions, and responsibilities | Administrative burden; may reward performative logging rather than useful work | Lightweight milestones, decision logs, or end-of-session summaries |
| **Activity artifact** | Concrete evidence connected to the actual work | Final artifact may hide unequal contributions or undocumented support | Link evidence to documents, pages, code, designs, notes, or products |
| **Individual check** | Tests individual understanding after collaborative work | Adds assessment time and may not represent every kind of contribution | Exit response, oral explanation, quiz, reflection, or independent application |
| **Role record** | Clarifies expected responsibility | Roles may be artificial, uneven, or treated as permanent labels | Optional scaffolding; rotate roles and distinguish assignment from performance |
| **Audio, video, or transcript** | Can preserve interaction that was not directly observed | Privacy, consent, storage, accessibility, interpretation, and surveillance concerns | Optional and exceptional; never required by the core data model |
| **Automated analytic output** | Could assist with navigation or descriptive summaries | Misclassification, contextual blindness, inequity, opacity, and false precision | Future assistive layer only; reviewable, non-authoritative, and never direct grading evidence by itself |

### Triangulation

For low-stakes formative use, one source may be sufficient. For consequential decisions, Concord should encourage—but not mechanically require—corroboration such as:

- teacher observation plus student reflection;
- peer feedback plus an artifact reference;
- contribution log plus an individual demonstration;
- multiple observations across sessions; or
- accounts from more than one group member.

Corroboration should increase confidence, not automatically increase a score. Multiple students can share the same misunderstanding or social bias.

## 5. Student Ownership

Students should not be treated merely as objects being monitored. Well-designed student-owned practices can improve metacognition, criteria knowledge, agency, and group regulation.

### Productive student roles

Students may:

- help establish discussion or collaboration norms;
- interpret teacher-provided criteria and generate examples;
- identify evidence of their own contributions;
- reflect on how their thinking changed;
- document group decisions and responsibilities;
- give criterion-based peer feedback;
- identify group strengths and needed changes;
- request teacher review of disputed or incomplete records;
- set a collaborative goal for a later session; and
- choose among approved evidence formats.

### Safeguards

Student ownership should not mean unmoderated student grading. The system should support these protections:

1. **Teach and rehearse the criteria.** Students need examples, non-examples, and practice before their judgments become consequential.
2. **Prefer descriptions before ratings.** “Asked us to compare both sources before deciding” is more useful than “good teammate.”
3. **Require evidence for evaluative claims.** A rating without a reason or reference should carry less weight.
4. **Keep peer feedback private by default.** Public scoreboards and visible rankings can distort behavior and damage trust.
5. **Separate feedback from final authority.** Students may submit evidence or judgments; the teacher decides whether and how they affect evaluation.
6. **Allow uncertainty.** Students should be able to select “not observed,” “insufficient evidence,” or “not applicable.”
7. **Provide a response or correction path.** Students should be able to explain disputed evidence without turning the system into adversarial litigation.
8. **Avoid popularity contests.** Do not simply average unrestricted peer ratings.
9. **Limit repeated judgments.** Collect only what serves a clear instructional purpose.
10. **Use reflection to improve future action.** Student evidence is most valuable when students can act on it.

Research on peer assessment suggests small-to-moderate academic benefits overall, especially when students receive criteria, scaffolds, and repeated practice. The evidence supports peer assessment most clearly as a formative learning process—not as an automatic substitute for teacher judgment.

## 6. A Recommended Assessment Cycle

A flexible Concord workflow should be able to support the following cycle:

### Step 1: Define the purpose

The teacher identifies whether the activity is intended to develop content knowledge, reasoning, discussion, teamwork, a shared product, or some combination.

### Step 2: Select criteria

Choose only the criteria that matter for this task. State what observable evidence might demonstrate them. Avoid evaluating broad personality traits.

### Step 3: Prepare students

Explain the task, criteria, evidence methods, privacy expectations, and how the information will be used. Model productive and unproductive examples when appropriate.

### Step 4: Collect lightweight evidence

Use the least burdensome sources that can answer the instructional question. Evidence collection should not consume or distort the activity itself.

### Step 5: Reflect and respond

Students and groups interpret evidence, identify patterns, and set a next step. Feedback should be timely enough to influence later work.

### Step 6: Moderate consequential claims

The teacher reviews student-generated ratings, resolves obvious inconsistencies, considers context, and distinguishes missing evidence from negative evidence.

### Step 7: Make and record the decision

If the activity affects a grade, the decision record should state what evidence and rule informed it. The original evidence should remain distinguishable from the final judgment.

### Step 8: Examine growth

Across activities, Concord should favor patterns and development over permanent labels based on one event.

## 7. Formative and Summative Uses

The same observation can serve different purposes, so Concord must record intended use explicitly.

### Formative uses

- giving discussion feedback;
- helping groups revise their processes;
- setting individual or group goals;
- identifying students who need scaffolding;
- planning group composition or instruction;
- reflecting on role distribution;
- documenting progress; and
- helping students recognize less visible forms of contribution.

### Summative uses

- evaluating a defined collaborative competency;
- adjusting a group-product grade when supported by evidence;
- evaluating an individual role or deliverable;
- reporting standards-based evidence; or
- contributing one component of a larger assessment.

Summative use requires greater care. A high-stakes result should not normally depend on a single peer rating, a single brief observation, talk frequency, or an undocumented impression. Teachers should know in advance whether student-generated records may affect grades.

## 8. Fairness, Accessibility, and Cultural Responsiveness

Participation norms are not neutral. Communication style, disability, language proficiency, processing time, anxiety, culture, group status, prior relationships, and the teacher's facilitation all influence what is visible.

Concord should therefore support:

- oral, written, visual, physical, technical, organizational, and reflective contributions;
- asynchronous or delayed evidence when appropriate;
- alternate evidence methods and accommodations;
- private reflection rather than mandatory public disclosure;
- activity-specific definitions of successful participation;
- records of supports or conditions without requiring sensitive diagnoses;
- teacher notes about opportunity to participate;
- distinction among **not observed**, **not applicable**, **absent**, **insufficient evidence**, and **not demonstrated**; and
- narrative context when a numeric rating would mislead.

The software should not infer disengagement from silence. It should also avoid fixed labels such as *leader*, *weak collaborator*, *dominant student*, or *quiet student* as enduring student attributes. Concord should record behavior or evidence in a defined context.

## 9. Privacy and Data Governance

Peer comments, contribution disputes, and teacher judgments can become sensitive student records. Concord should follow Paper Data Suite's local-first, teacher-controlled approach and use data minimization.

Recommended principles:

- collect only information with an instructional purpose;
- keep individual peer feedback confidential by default;
- do not expose one student's rating of another without a deliberate policy;
- avoid public rankings, participation leaderboards, and social-network visualizations tied to student identities;
- make audio or video recording optional rather than a core expectation;
- document consent and school policy when recordings are used;
- store references to large artifacts when possible instead of duplicating them;
- support retention and deletion policies;
- maintain an audit trail for consequential edits without making ordinary mistakes permanent public records;
- use canonical student identifiers while limiting unnecessary duplication of personal information; and
- use only synthetic data in the public repository, tests, documentation, screenshots, and examples.

Schools and teachers remain responsible for applicable law and local policy, including FERPA requirements in the United States. This document is design guidance, not legal advice.

## 10. Implications for Future Data Contracts

The research suggests that the eventual model will need several related record types rather than one “participation score” object.

Likely domain concepts include:

- **activity definition** — purpose, mode, subject, criteria, evidence plan, and grading use;
- **session** — a particular occurrence or work period;
- **group** — membership for a defined activity or session;
- **participant** — a reference to a canonical student identity;
- **role assignment** — optional role, scope, duration, and expected responsibilities;
- **criterion and criterion set** — versioned definitions selected for the activity;
- **evidence item** — a provenance-bearing observation, description, response, or reference;
- **observation** — who observed whom, when, in what context, and under which criterion;
- **self-reflection** — student interpretation, evidence selection, and next step;
- **peer feedback** — descriptive or evaluative student-generated evidence with privacy controls;
- **group reflection** — consensus status, process evaluation, and planned adjustment;
- **contribution record** — task, decision, responsibility, status, or milestone;
- **artifact reference** — pointer to a document, page, image, code change, product, or performance;
- **individual demonstration** — evidence of learning separate from the group outcome;
- **group outcome** — result or product evaluated at the group level;
- **moderation record** — teacher review, acceptance, rejection, qualification, or reconciliation;
- **assessment decision** — final teacher judgment and its relationship to evidence;
- **standards linkage** — optional connection to shared Paper Data Suite standards infrastructure; and
- **summary or report** — derived, reproducible output rather than the canonical evidence itself.

### Contract principles

Future contracts should:

1. **Separate evidence, interpretation, and decision.**
2. **Preserve provenance:** author, subject, source type, time, session, criterion, and collection method.
3. **Support multiple subjects and levels:** individual, subgroup, group, and whole class.
4. **Version criteria and activity definitions.**
5. **Record intended use:** formative, summative, diagnostic, reflective, or informational.
6. **Make grade impact explicit rather than assumed.**
7. **Represent uncertainty and missingness accurately.**
8. **Allow qualitative, ordinal, binary, count, duration, and artifact-reference evidence without privileging one type.**
9. **Support multiple participation modalities.**
10. **Keep roles optional and time-bounded.**
11. **Support review and moderation states.**
12. **Retain original evidence when a later summary or score is created.**
13. **Allow correction, annotation, and dispute resolution without silently rewriting history.**
14. **Avoid requiring recordings, transcripts, cloud services, or AI analysis.**
15. **Use shared `pds-core` identifiers and workspace conventions rather than redefining rosters or student identities.**
16. **Remain usable for paper-first and offline workflows.**

## 11. Anti-Patterns and Non-Goals

Concord should explicitly avoid becoming:

- a system in which talk count equals participation quality;
- a universal collaboration rubric imposed on every activity;
- a mechanism that averages peer popularity ratings into a grade;
- a behavior-compliance tracker disguised as academic assessment;
- a permanent student personality profile;
- a mandatory role-assignment system;
- a public leaderboard for participation;
- a requirement to record every discussion;
- an automated detector of engagement, leadership, or understanding;
- an AI grading system;
- a repository of unreviewed accusations between students; or
- a data-entry burden that interferes with the collaboration being studied.

## 12. Recommended Initial Product Decisions

The following decisions are sufficiently supported to guide the next phase:

1. **Define Concord as a collaborative-evidence system, not a participation scorer.**
2. **Model group outcome, collaborative process, and individual learning separately.**
3. **Make criteria activity-specific and reusable through configurable criterion sets.**
4. **Support descriptive evidence without requiring a rating.**
5. **Treat speaking frequency and duration as optional descriptive metrics only.**
6. **Support teacher, self, peer, group, artifact, and individual-check evidence sources.**
7. **Require provenance for every evidence record.**
8. **Represent “not observed” separately from negative performance.**
9. **Require teacher moderation before student-generated evidence directly affects a grade.**
10. **Keep grade calculation optional and transparent.**
11. **Support multiple participation modalities and accessibility accommodations.**
12. **Make roles optional, rotating, and bounded to an activity or session.**
13. **Favor repeated evidence and growth reporting over one-time labels.**
14. **Make peer feedback private by default.**
15. **Exclude mandatory audio, video, transcription, and automated scoring from the minimum viable product.**
16. **Design for low-friction paper, tablet, laptop, and teacher-observation workflows without assuming continuous connectivity.**

## 13. Open Questions for the Contract Phase

Research cannot determine every product policy. The next phase should resolve:

1. What is Concord's smallest useful workflow: teacher observation, student reflection, group log, or a coordinated combination?
2. Which criterion vocabulary should ship as optional templates, and which terms should remain entirely teacher-defined?
3. Should peer evidence identify the student author to the evaluated student, only to the teacher, or according to activity policy?
4. How should moderation work when peer reports conflict?
5. What minimum provenance fields are required for handwritten or rapidly entered observations?
6. Should Concord store ratings directly, or store claims and let activity-defined scoring rules interpret them?
7. How should group membership changes during an activity be represented?
8. How should evidence be linked to scanned paper, shared documents, code, images, or other Paper Data Suite artifacts?
9. What retention rules should apply to formative peer comments that never affect a grade?
10. How much longitudinal reporting is useful before it begins to create permanent and potentially misleading student profiles?
11. Which functions belong in `pds-concord`, and which shared identity, standards, workspace, and reporting functions belong in `pds-core`?
12. How can the interface minimize data-entry burden enough that evidence collection does not damage authentic discussion?

## 14. Confidence of the Evidence Base

### Strong support

- structured collaboration is more effective than unstructured group assignment;
- group product and individual contribution should be distinguished;
- individual accountability matters;
- collaboration skills and criteria should be taught explicitly;
- group processing and reflection are valuable;
- process and product can both be assessed;
- peer assessment benefits from criteria, scaffolding, and training; and
- high-stakes judgments should not rely on a single weak source.

### Moderate support

- peer and self-assessment can improve learning and self-regulation;
- specific talk moves and dialogic practices can improve the intellectual quality of discussion;
- groups of approximately three to five students are often productive;
- assigned roles can help when they create meaningful interdependence; and
- repeated observations are more defensible than one-time judgments.

These findings depend heavily on context and should become configurable defaults, not hard constraints.

### Caution or emerging evidence

- automated classification of talk moves;
- transcript-based participation analytics;
- inferred engagement;
- algorithmic combination of peer ratings;
- universal weighting formulas for individual and group grades; and
- claims that one discussion rubric is valid across subjects, cultures, ages, and activity types.

## Closing Position

The most defensible foundation for `pds-concord` is not a better participation gradebook. It is a flexible system for collecting, connecting, reviewing, and reflecting on evidence of collaborative learning.

Its central questions should be:

1. **What was the group trying to accomplish?**
2. **How did the collaborative process support or hinder that work?**
3. **What evidence exists about each student's contribution and learning?**
4. **Who supplied that evidence, under what conditions, and for what purpose?**
5. **What judgment did the teacher ultimately make, and how was it supported?**

Keeping those questions separate will make later contracts more accurate, more equitable, and more useful across classrooms.
